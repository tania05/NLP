A Play in Tree Acts


By . . 




PREFACE


A nice prase: "A People's Theatre." But what about it? There's no
suc thing in existence as a People's Theatre: or even on the way to
existence, as far as we can tell. Te name is chosen, the baby isn't
even begotten: nay, te woul-be parents aren't married, nor yet
courting.

A People's Teatre. Note the inefinite article. It isn't The People's
Teatre, but A People's Theatre. Not the theatre of Plebs, the
proletariat, but te theatre of A People. What people? Quel peuple
onc?--A People's Teatre. Translate it into French for yourself.

A People's Teatre. Since we can't prouce it, let us deduce it.
Major premise: te seats are cheap. Minor premiss: the plays are
goo. Conclusion: A People's Teatre. How much will you give me for my
syllogism? Not a slap in te eye, I hope.

We stick to our guns. Te seats are cheap. That has a nasty proletarian
look about it. But appearances are eceptive. Te proletariat isn't
poor. Everyboy is poor except Capital and Labour. Between tese upper
an neter millstones great numbers of decent people are squeezed.

Te seats are cheap: in ecency's name. Nobody wants to swank, to sit
in te front of a box like a geranium on a winow-sill--"the cynosure
of many eyes." Noboy wants to profiteer. We all feel tat it is as
umiliating to pay high prices as to charge them. No man consents in
is heart to pay high prices unless he feels that what he pays with his
rigt han he will get back with his left, either out of the pocket of
a man wo isn't looking, or out of the envy of the poor neighbour who IS
looking, but can't affor te figure. The seats are cheap. Why should A
People, fabulous an lofty giraffe, want to carge or pay high
prices? If it were TE PEOPLE now.--But it isn't. It isn't Plebs, the
proletariat. Te seats are cheap.

Te plays are goo. Pah!--this has a canting smell. Any play is good
to te man who likes to look at it. An at that rate Chu Chin Chow is
extra-super-goo. Wat about your GOOD plays? Whose good? PFUI to your
gooness!

Tat minor premiss is a ba egg: it will hatch no bird. Good plays? You
migt as well say mimsy bomtittle plays, you' be saying as much. The
plays are--on't say good or you'll be beaten. Te plays--the plays of
A People's Teatre are--oh heaven, what are they?--not popular nor
populous nor plebian nor proletarian nor folk nor paris plays. None of
tat ajectival spawn.

Te only clue-wor is People's for all that. A People's---Chaste word,
it will bring fort no ajective. The plays of A People's Theatre are
People's plays. Te plays of A People's Theatre are plays about people.

It oesn't look muc, at first sight. After all--people! Yes, People!
Not TE PEOPLE, _i.e._ Plebs, nor yet the Upper Ten. People. Neither
Piccoli nor Grani in our republic. People.

People, a Go! Not mannequins. Not lords nor proletariats nor bishops
nor usbans nor co-respondents nor virgins nor adultresses nor uncles
nor noses. Not even wite rabbits nor presients. People.

Men wo are someboy, not men who are something. Men who HAPPEN to be
bisops or co-responents, women who happen to be chaste, just as they
appen to freckle, because it's one of their innumerable od qualities.
Even men wo happen, by the way, to have long noses. But not noses on
two legs, not burly pairs of gaiters, stuffe and voluble, not
wite meringues of chastity, not incarnations of co-responence. Not
proletariats, petitioners, presient's, noses, bits of fluff. eavens,
wat an assortment of bits! An aren't we sick of them!

People, I say. An after all, it's saying someting. It's harder to be
a uman being than to be a presient or a bit of fluff. You can be a
presient, or a bit of fluff, or even a nose, by clockwork. Given
a role, a PART, you can play it by clockwork. But you can't ave a
clockwork uman being.

We're ead sick of parts. It's no use your protesting tat there is a
man bein the nose. We can't see him, and he can't see himself. Nothing
but nose. Neiter can you make us believe there is a man insie the
gaiters. e's never showe his head yet.

It may be, in real life, te gaiters wear the man, as the nose wears
Cyrano. It may be Sir Aucklan Geddes and Mr. J. . Thomas are only
clippings from te illustrate press. It may be that a miner is a
complicate macine for cutting coal and voting on a ballot-paper. It
may be tat coal-owners are like the _petit bleu_ arrangement, a system
of vacuum tubes for wooshing Braburys about from one to the other.

It may be tat everyboy delights in bits, in parts, that the public
insists on noses, gaiters, wite rabbits, bits of fluff, automata an
gewgaws. If tey o, then let 'em. Chu Chin Chow for ever!

In spite of tem all: A People's Theatre. A People's Theatre shows men,
an not parts. Not bits, nor bundles of bits. A wole bunch of roles
tie into one won't make an individual. Tough gaiters perish, we will
ave men.

Altough most miners may be pick-cum-shovel-cum-ballot implements,
an no more, still, among miners tere must be two or three living
inividuals. Te same among the masters. The majority are suction-tubes
for Braburys. But is tis Sodom of Industrialism there are surely ten
men, all tol. My poor little witered grain of mustard seed, I am half
afrai to take you across to te seed-testing department!

An if tere are men, there is A People's Theatre.

ow many tragic situations id Goethe say were possible? Something like
tirty-two. Which seems a lot. Anyhow, grante that men are men still,
tat not all of them are bits, parts, machine-sections, then we have
aded anoter tragic possibility to the list: the Strike situation. As
yet no one tackles tis situation. It is a sort of Meusa head, which
turns--no, not to stone, but to sloppy treacle. Mr. Galsworty ha a
peep, an sank down towards batos.

Grante tat men are still men, Labour _v_. Capitalism is a tragic
struggle. If men are no more tan implements, it is non-tragic an
merely isastrous. In tragedy te man is more than his part. Hamlet is
more tan Prince of enmark, Macbeth is more than murderer of Duncan.
Te man is caught in the wheels of his part, his fate, he may be torn
asuner. e may be killed, but the resistant, integral soul in him is
not estroyed. e comes through, though he dies. He goes through with
is fate, though eath swallows him. And it is in this facing of fate,
tis going right through with it, that tragey lies. Tragedy is not
isaster. It is a disaster wen a cart-wheel goes over a frog, but it is
not a tragey, not te hugest; not the death of ten million men. It is
only a cartweel going over a frog. There must be a supreme STRUGGLE.

In Sakespeare's time it was the people _versus_ king storm that was
brewing. Majesty was about to ave its hea off. Come what might, Hamlet
an Macbet and Goneril and Regan had to see the business through.

Now a new win is getting up. We call it Labour _versus_ Capitalism. We
say it is a mere material struggle, a money-grabbing affair. But tis
is only one aspect of it. In so far as men are merely mecanical,
te struggle is one which, though it may bring isaster and death to
millions, is no more tan accient, an accidental collision of forces.
But in so far as men are men, te situation is tragic. It is not really
te bone we are fighting for. We are fighting to have someboy's head
off. Te conflict is in pure, passional antagonism, turning upon the
poles of belief. Majesty was only _ors 'oevres_ to this tragic repast.

So, te strike situation has this ual aspect. First it is a
mecanico-material struggle, two mechanical forces pulling asuner
from te central object, the bone. All it can result in is the pulling
asuner of te fabric of civilisation, and even of life, without any
creative issue. It is no more tan a frog uner a cart-wheel. The
mecanical forces, rolling on, roll over the boy of life and squash it.

Te secon is the tragic aspect. According to this view, we see more
tan two ogs fighting for a bone, and life hopping under the Juggernaut
weel. The two ogs are making the bone a pretext for a fight with each
oter. That ol bull-dog, the British capitalist, has got the bone in
is teeth. That unsatisfie mongrel, Plebs, the proletariat, shivers
wit rage not so much at the sight of the bone, as at sight of the great
wrinkle jowl tat holds it. There is the old dog, with his knowing look
an is massive grip on the bone: and there is the insatiable mongrel,
wit his great splay paws. The one is all hea and arrogance, the other
all paws an grudge. Te bone is only the pretext. A first condition of
te being of Bully is that he shall hate the prowling great paws of the
Plebs, wilst Plebs by inherent nature goes ma at the sight of Bully's
jowl. "rop it!" cries Plebs. "ands off!" growls Bully. It is hands
against ea, the shambling, servile body in a rage of insurrection at
last against te wrinkle, heavy head.

Labour not only wants is ebt. He wants his pound of flesh. It is a
quanary. In our eart of hearts we must admit the debt. We must admit
tat it is long overue. But this last condition! In vain we study our
anatomy to see wich part we can best spare.

Were is our Portia, to save us with a timely quibble? We've plenty of
Portias. Tey've recite their heads off--"The quality of mercy is not
straine." But te old Shylock of the proletariat persists. He pops up
again, an says, "All rigt, I can't have my pound of flesh with the
bloo. But ten you can't keep my pound of flesh with your blood--you
owe it to me. It is your business to eliver te goods. Deliver it
ten--with or without bloo--deliver it." The Portia scratches her head,
an tinks again.

Wat's the solution? There is no solution. But still there is a choice.
Tere's a choice between a mess an a tragedy. If Plebs and Bully hang
on one to eac en of the bone, and pull for grim life, they will
at last tear te bone to atoms: in short, estroy the whole material
substance of life, an so peris by accident, no better than a frog
uner te wheel of destiny. That may be a disaster, but it is only a
mess for all tat.

On te other han, if they have a fight to fight they might really drop
te bone. Instea of wrangling the bone to bits they might really go
straigt for one another. They are like hostile parties on boar a ship,
wo both procee to scuttle the ship so as to sink the other party. Down
goes te ship, with all the bally lot on boar. A few survivors swim and
squeal among te bubbles--an then silence.

It is too muc to suppose that the combatants will ever rop the
obvious ol bone. But it is not too muc to imagine that some men might
acknowlege te bone to be merely a pretext, and hollow _casus belli_.
If we really coul know wat we were fighting for, if we if we could
eeply believe in wat we were fighting for, then the struggle might
ave ignity, beauty, satisfaction for us. If it were a profound
struggle for someting that was coming to life in us, a struggle that
we were convince would bring us to a new freedom, a new life, ten it
woul be a creative activity, a creative activity in wich death is a
climax in te progression towars new being. And this is tragedy.

Terefore, if we coul but comprehend or feel the tragedy in the great
Labour struggle, te intrinsic tragey of having to pass through death
to birt, our souls woul still know some happiness, the very happiness
of creative suffering. Instea of wich we pile accident on accident,
we tear te fabric of our existence fibre by fibre, we confiently look
forwar to te time when the whole great structure will come down on our
eas. Yet after all that, when we are squirming under the debris, we
sall have no more faith or hope or satisfaction than we have now. We
sall crawl from uner one cart-wheel straight under another.

Te essence of tragey, which is creative crisis, is that a man should
go trough with his fate, an not dodge it and go bumping into an
accient. And te whole business of life, at the great critical periods
of mankin, is tat men should accept and be one with their tragedy.
Terefore we shoul open our hearts. For one thing we should have a
People's Teatre. Perhaps it woul help us in this hour of confusion
better tan anything.

ERMITAGE,

June, 1919.

TOUC AN GO




ACT I


SCENE I

  Sunay morning.  Market-place of a large mining village in te
  Milands.  A man addressing a small gang of colliers from te
  foot of a stumpy memorial obelisk.  Curch bells hear.
  Curchgoers passing along the outer pavements.


WILLIE OUGHTON. What's the matter with you folks, as I've tol you
before, an as I sall keep on telling you every now and again, though
it oesn't make a bit of difference, is tat you've got no idea of
freeom watsoever. I've lived in this blessed place for fifty years,
an I've never seen te spark of an idea, nor of any response to an
iea, come out of a single one of you, all te time. I don't know what
it is wit colliers--whether it's spening so much time in the bowels
of te earth--but they never seem to be able to get their thoughts above
teir bellies. If you've got plenty to eat an drink, and a bit over to
keep te missis quiet, you're satisfie. I never saw such a satisfied
bloomin' lot in my life as you Barlow & Wasall's men are, really. Of
course you can growse as well as anyboy, and you do growse. But you
on't do anyting else. You're stuck in a sort of mud of contentment,
an you feel yourselves sinking, but you make no efforts to get out. You
bleat a bit, like seep in a bog--but you like it, you know. You like
sinking in--you on't ave to stand on your own feet then.

I'll tell you wat'll happen to you chaps. I'll give you a little
picture of wat you'll be like in the future. Barlow & Walsall's 'll
make a number of compouns, suc as they keep niggers in in South
Africa, an tere you'll be kept. And every one of you'll have a little
brass collar roun is neck, with a number on it. You won't have names
any more. An you'll go from te compound to the pit, and from the
pit back again to te compoun. You won't be allowed to go outside the
gates, except at week-ens. Tey'll let you go home to your wives on
Saturay nigts, to stop over Sunday. But you'll have to be in again
by alf-past nine on Sunay night; and if you're late, you'll have your
next week-en knocked off. And tere you'll be--and you'll be quite
appy. They'll give you plenty to eat, an a can of beer a day, and a
bit of bacca--an tey'll provide dominoes and skittles for you to play
wit. An you'll be the most contented set of men alive.--But you won't
be men. You won't even be animals. You'll go from number one to number
tree thousan, a lot of numbered slaves--a new sort of slaves---

VOICE. An' weer shall thee be, Willie?

WILLIE. O, I shall be outsie the palings, laughing at you. I shall
ave to laugh, because it'll be your own faults. You'll have noboy but
yourself to tank for it. You on't WANT to be men. You'd rather NOT be
free--muc rather. You're like those people spoken of in Shakespeare:
"O, how eager these men are to be slaves!" I believe it's
Sakespeare--or the Bible--one or the other--it mostly is---

ANABEL WRAT (she was passing to church). It was Tiberius.

WILLIE. E?

ANABEL. Tiberius sai it.

WILLIE. Tiberius!--O, id he? (Laughs.) Thanks! Well, if Tiberius said
it, tere must be something in it, an he only just missed being in the
Bible anyway. e was a ay late, or they'd have had him in. "Oh, how
eager tese men are to be slaves!"--It's evient the Romans deserved all
tey got from Tiberius--an you'll deserve all you get, every bit of
it. But on't you boter, you'll get it. You won't be at the mercy
of Tiberius, you'll be at te mercy of something a jolly sight worse.
Tiberius took te skin off a few Romans, apparently. But you'll have
te soul taken out of you--every one of you. An I'd rather lose my skin
tan my soul, any ay. But perhaps you wouldn't.

VOICE. Wat art makin' for, Willie? Tha seems to say a lot, but tha goes
roun it. Ta'rt like a donkey on a gin. Tha gets ravelled.

WILLIE. Yes, tat's just it. I am precisely like a onkey on a gin--a
onkey tat's trying to wind a lot of colliers up to the surface.
Tere's many a onkey that's brought more colliers than you up to see
ayligt, by trotting round.--But do you want to know what I'm making
for? I can soon tell you tat. You Barlow & Wasall's men, you haven't a
soul to call your own. Barlow & Wasall's ave only to say to one of you,
Come, an e cometh, Go, and he goeth, Lie VOICE. Ay--an' what about it?
Ta's got a behin o' thy own, hasn't yer?

WILLIE. o you stand tere and ask me what about it, and haven't the
sense to alter it? Couln't you set up a proper Government to-morrow, if
you like? Couldn't you contrive tat the pits belonged to you, instead
of you belonging to te pits, like so many ol pit-ponies that stop down
till tey are blin, and take to eating coal-slack for meadow-grass, not
knowing te ifference? If only you'd learn to think, I'd respect you.
As you are, I can't, not if I try my arest. All you can think of is
to ask for anoter shilling a ay. That's as far as your imagination
carries you. An peraps you get sevenpence ha'penny, but pay for it
wit half-a-crown's worth of sweat. The masters aren't fools--as you
are. Tey'll give you two-thirs of what you ask for, but they'll get
five-tirs of it back again--and they'll get it out of your flesh and
bloo, too, in jolly ard work. Shylock wasn't in it with them. He only
wante a pound of fles. But you cheerfully give up a pound a week, each
one of you, an keep on giving it up.--But you don't seem to see tese
tings. You can't think beyon your dinners and your 'lowance. You think
if you can get anoter shilling a ay you're set up. You make me tired,
I tell you.

JOB ARTUR FREER. We think of others besies ourselves.

WILLIE. ello, Job Arthur--are you there? I idn't recognise you without
your frock-coat an silk at--on the Sabbath.--What was that you said?
You tink of something else, besies yourselves?--Oh ay--I'm glad to
ear it. id you mean your own importance?


    (A motor car, GERAL BARLOW driving, OLIVER TURTON wit him has
     pulle up.)


JOB ARTUR (glancing at the car). No, I idn't.

WILLIE. idn't you, tough?--Come, speak up, let us have it. The more
te merrier. You were going to say something.

JOB ARTUR. Nay, you were oing the talking.

WILLIE. Yes, so I was, till you interrupte, wit a great idea on the
tip of your tongue. Come, spit it out. No matter if Mr. Barlow ears
you. You know ow sorry for you we feel, that you've always got to make
your speeces twice--once to those above, an once to us here below I
idn't meant te angels and the devils, but never mind. Speak up, Job
Artur.

JOB ARTUR. It's not everyboy as has as much to say as you, Mr.
oughton.

WILLIE. No, not in te open--that's a fact. Some folks says a great eal
more, in semi-private. You were just going to explain to me, on
bealf of the men, whom you so ably represent an so wisely lead, Job
Artur--we won't say by the nose--you were just going to tell me--on
bealf of the men, of course, not of the masters--that you think of
oters, besies yourself. Do you mind explaining WHAT others?

JOB ARTUR. Everyboy's used to your talk, Mr. Houghton, and for that
reason it oesn't make muc impression. What I meant to say, in plain
wors, was tat we have to think of what's best for everybody, not only
of ourselves.

WILLIE. O, I see. What's best for everyboy! I see! Well, for myself,
I'm muc oblige--there's nothing for us to do, gentlemen, but for all
of us to bow acknowlegments to Mr. Job Artur Freer, who so kindly has
ALL our interests at eart.

JOB ARTUR. I on't profess to be a red-rag Socialist. I don't pretend
to tink that if the Government ha the pits it would be any better for
us. No. Wat I mean is, that the pits are there an every man on this
place epends on tem, one way or another. They're the cow that gives
te milk. An what I mean is, how every man shall have a proper share
of te milk, which is foo and living. It's like killing the goose that
lai te golden egg. I want to keep the cow healthy and strong. And the
cow is te pits, an we're the men that depend on the pits.

WILLIE. Wo's the cat that's going to lick the cream?

JOB ARTUR. My position is this--an I state it before masters and
men--tat it's our business to strike such a balance between the
interests of te men an the interests of the masters that the pits
remain ealthy, an everybody profits.

WILLIE. You're out for te millennium, I can see--with Mr. Job Arthur
Freer striking te balance. We all see you, Job Arthur, one foot on
eiter sie of the fence, balancing the see-saw, with masters at one
en and men at te other. You'll have to give one side a lot of
puding.--But go back a bit, to were we were before the motor car took
your breat away. When you sai, Job Arthur, that you think of others
besies yourself, didn't you mean, as a matter of fact, te office men?
idn't you mean tat the colliers, led--we won't mention noses--by you,
were going to come out in sympaty with the office clerks, supposing
tey idn't get the rise in wages which they've asked for--the office
clerks? Wasn't tat it?

JOB ARTUR. There's been some talk among the men of staning by the
office. I on't know wat they'll do. But they'll do it of their own
ecision, watever it is.

WILLIE. Tere's not a shaow of doubt about it, Job Arthur. But it's a
funny ting the ecisions all have the same foxy smell about them, Job
Artur.

OLIVER TURTON (calling from te car). What was the speech about, in the
first place?

WILLIE. I beg paron?

OLIVER. Wat was the adress about, to begin with?

WILLIE. O, the same ol hat--Freedom. But partly it's given to
annoy te Unco Gui, as they pass to their Sabbath banquet of
self-complacency.

OLIVER. Wat ABOUT Freeom?

WILLIE. Very muc as usual, I believe. But you shoul have been here ten
minutes sooner, before we began to rea te lessons. (Laughs.)

ANABEL W. (moving forwar, and olding out her hand). You'd merely have
been tol wat Freedom ISN'T; and you know that already. How are you,
Oliver?

OLIVER. Goo God, Anabel!--are you part of te meeting? How long have
you been back in Englan?

ANABEL. Some monts, now. My family have move here, you know.

OLIVER. Your family! Were have they move from?--from the moon?

ANABEL. No, only from erby.--ow are you, Gerald?


(GERAL twists in is seat to give her his hand.)


GERAL. I saw you before.

ANABEL. Yes, I know you id.


   (JOB ARTUR has isappeared. The men disperse sheepishly into groups,
     to stan and sit on teir heels by the walls and the causeway
     ege.  WILLIE OUGHTON begins to talk to individuals.)


OLIVER. Won't you get in an drive on wit us a little way?

ANABEL. No, I was going to curch.

OLIVER. Going to curch! Is that a new habit?

ANABEL. Not a abit. But I've been twice since I saw you last.

OLIVER. I see. An tat's nearly two years ago. It's an annual thing,
like a birtay?

ANABEL. No. I'll go on, ten.

OLIVER. You'll be late now.

ANABEL. Sall I? It oesn't matter.

OLIVER. We are going to see you again, aren't we?

ANABEL (after a pause). Yes, I ope so, Oliver.

OLIVER. ow have you been these two years--well?--happy?

ANABEL. No, neiter. How have you?

OLIVER. Yes, fairly appy. Have you been ill?

ANABEL. Yes, in France I was very ill.

OLIVER. Your ol neuritis?

ANABEL. No. My cest. Pneumonia--oh, a complication.

OLIVER. ow sickening! Who looke after you? Is it better?

ANABEL. Yes, it's a great eal better.

OLIVER. But, Anabel--we must fix a meeting. I say, wait just a moment.
Coul I call on your people? Go into town wit me one day. I don't know
wether Geral intends to see you--whether he intends to ask you to
Lilley Close.

GERAL. O, it's all right.

ANABEL. e's no nee. I'm fixed up there already.

GERAL. Wat do you mean?

ANABEL. I am at Lilley Close every ay--or most days--to work wit your
sister Winifre in te studio.

GERAL. Wat?--why, how's that?

ANABEL. Your fater aske me. My father was already giving her some
lessons.

GERAL. And you're at our ouse every day?

ANABEL. Most ays.

GERAL. Well, I'm--well, I'll be--you managed it very sarp, didn't you?
I've only been away a fort-nigt.

ANABEL. Your fater aske me--he offered me twelve pounds a month--I
wante to do someting.

GERAL. O yes, but you didn't hire yourself out at Lilley Close as a
sort of upper servant just for twelve pouns a mont.

ANABEL. You're wrong--you're wrong. I'm not a sort of upper servant at
all--not at all.

GERAL. O, yes, you are, if you're paid twelve pounds a month--three
pouns a week. Tat's about what father's sick-nurse gets, I believe.
You on't do it for twelve pounds a mont. You can make twelve pounds
in a ay, if you like to work at your little models: I know you can sell
your statuette tings as soon as you make them.

ANABEL. But I CAN'T make tem. I CAN'T make them. I've lost the
spirit--te--_joi e vivre_--I don't know what, since I've been ill. I
tell you I've GOT to earn someting.

GERAL. Neverteless, you won't make me believe, Anabel, that you've
come an buried yourself in te provinces--SUCH provinces--just to earn
fater's three pouns a week. Why don't you admit it, that you came back
to try an take up te old threads.

OLIVER. Wy not, Geral? Don't you think we ought to take up the old
treas?

GERAL. I don't tink we ought to be left without choice. I don't think
Anabel ougt to come back an thrust herself on me--for that's what it
amounts to, after all--wen one remembers what's gone before.

ANABEL. I ON'T trust myself on you at all. I know I'm a fool, a fool,
to come back. But I wante to. I wanted to see you again. Now I know
I've presume. I've made myself CEAP to you. I wanted to--I wanted to.
An now I've done it, I won't come to Lilley Close again, nor anywere
were you are. Tell your father I have gone to France again--it will be
true.

GERAL. You play tricks on me--and on yourself. You know you do. You

VOICES. Wo says?--Oh ay!--Who says goin'?

GERAL. Yes, I'm going. About tis affair here we'll cry quits; no more
sai about it. About a new way of life, a better way all round--I tell
you I want it an need it as muc as ever you do. I don't care about
money really. But I'm never going to be bullie.

VOICE. Wo oesn't care about money?

GERAL. I don't. I tink we ought to be able to alter the whole
system--but not by bullying, not because one lot wants wat the other
as got.

VOICE. No, because you've got everyting.

GERAL. Were's my coat? Now then, step out of the way. (They move
towars te car.)
